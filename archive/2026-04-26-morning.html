Trump's Cancellation: The Structural Read

Trump scrapped the Witkoff/Kushner Pakistan trip Saturday afternoon, hours after Araghchi left Islamabad. The stated reasons are analytically significant:

"There is tremendous infighting and confusion within their 'leadership.' Nobody knows who is in charge, including them." — Donald Trump, Truth Social, Apr 25
"We're not going to spend 15 hours in airplanes all the time going back and forth to be giving a document that was not good enough." — Trump, CNBC

This is the US president publicly adopting the faction-deadlock framework that has been the core of our analysis since Day 1. "Nobody knows who is in charge" is not rhetoric — it is an accurate description of the post-Khamenei IRGC-dominated decision-making structure. The analytical implication: Trump does not believe there is a counterparty capable of making a deal. Until that changes, the US posture is maximum pressure with zero urgency to negotiate.

On Iran's proposal: "Offered a lot, but not enough." This confirms Iran did table a substantive offer through Pakistan. But Trump's "all they have to do is call" framing sets an explicit gate: direct contact. The IRGC opposes direct contact. This is structurally unsatisfiable under current factional conditions.

Araghchi's Three-Country Sprint

While Trump was canceling, Araghchi was activating alternative diplomatic channels:

  • Islamabad (Apr 24-25): All-night session with Army Chief Munir + FM Dar + Interior Min Naqvi
  • Muscat (Apr 26): Met Sultan Haitham bin Tariq at al-Baraka Palace. Discussed "regional developments" and mediation. (PressTV)
  • Back to Islamabad (Apr 26): Pakistan "racing to save negotiations." (Washington Times) Next stop: Moscow.

The Oman stop is the most significant detail. Oman traditionally mediates Iran-US back-channels (this is how the JCPOA began). Araghchi going to Oman after Trump's cancellation signals an attempt to establish an alternative pathway. Sultan Haitham emphasized "prioritizing dialogue and diplomacy." Whether Oman can succeed where Pakistan couldn't is the new question.

Goldman Sachs: The New End-State Framework

Jared Cohen, co-head of the Goldman Sachs Global Institute, published the most analytically important reframe of the week (Fortune, Apr 25):

"Unless Iran's regime collapses, the Strait of Hormuz will never be open like it was before the war. You may have traffic flowing through, but the Iranians will likely maintain partial or unilateral control." — Jared Cohen, Goldman Sachs

Why this matters: The analytical community (and prediction markets) have been treating Hormuz as binary — open or closed. Goldman is arguing the end-state is a third option: managed instability with partial traffic under Iranian unilateral control. Iran retains 1,000-2,000 missiles. The toll regime becomes permanent. Insurance premiums stay elevated. This means even a "deal" doesn't restore pre-war oil supply chains.

The bear case on Goldman's take: Cohen may be overweighting Iran's current leverage. If Pezeshkian's economic collapse timeline holds (day 16 of 21-28), the IRGC may be forced to concede more than Goldman assumes. The IEA emergency stockpile release buys time for diplomacy. And the US has three carrier groups — Iranian "leverage" is contingent on the US tolerating it. But structurally, Goldman is probably right that pre-war Hormuz is gone.
