The All-Night Session

Araghchi landed in Islamabad to the escort of Pakistani fighter jets — a deliberate show of protocol — and was greeted at the air base by Field Marshal Asim Munir, FM Ishaq Dar, and Interior Minister Mohsin Naqvi. They plunged into talks that lasted until dawn, roughly 5 hours. (DAWN)

Saturday morning, Araghchi met Munir again along with Naqvi. Araghchi praised Munir's "commendable efforts" and "valuable endeavors" for the ceasefire. Munir "announced Pakistan's readiness to continue mediation until a result is achieved." (Euronews, MEMO)

The structural read: Five hours of overnight engagement suggests the content is real. Pakistan's fighter-jet protocol signals they take the mediation seriously. But Iran has explicitly ruled out direct US talks — Araghchi's spokesperson said "any talks between Tehran and Washington would be indirect, with messages conveyed to Pakistani officials instead." This is proximity diplomacy, not bilateral negotiation. It's slower, more prone to miscommunication, and harder to produce breakthroughs. Round 1 was 21 hours of direct face-to-face and still failed on 2 of 10 points.

Timeline Slippage

Witkoff and Kushner, originally announced for Saturday, are now expected Sunday. The actual US-Iran meeting — through Pakistani intermediaries — may not happen until Monday. (CBS) Vance is not attending but remains "on standby." The binary window that was supposed to resolve this weekend now extends into early next week.

The Physical-vs-Political Timeline Gap

This is the most important new structural variable. Pentagon assessments: mine-clearing could take 6 months even after a deal is reached. (NPR) Baker Hughes CFO Ahmed Moghal: guidance assumes conflict through end of June, Hormuz "may not be fully operational until the second half of the year." (Benzinga) Dallas Fed Energy Survey: ~80% of oil and gas executives expect Hormuz not reopening until August or later. (Seeking Alpha)

Implication: Even an optimistic political deal signed next week does not immediately fix Hormuz. 10% of global oil and 20% of global LNG remain offline (Baker Hughes CEO Simonelli). The oil risk premium persists through summer regardless of diplomacy. This breaks the simple "deal → oil crash → entry zones reopen" thesis from Apr 8.

New Sanctions Pressure

US Treasury sanctioned a Chinese refinery and approximately 40 shipping firms accused of helping Iran circumvent oil export restrictions. (NPR) This adds a new layer to the China dimension — Beijing's support infrastructure for Iranian oil exports is being systematically targeted.
