London Coalition: Real but Slow

The UK MOD’s Permanent Joint Headquarters at Northwood hosted 30+ nation military planners in a two-day conference. 12+ countries said they would join an international mission led by Britain and France to protect shipping in the Strait of Hormuz. Military planners worked to turn the earlier Paris diplomatic summit (51 countries) into a detailed operational plan.

The critical qualifier: “when conditions permit.” This is coalition formation for the post-hostilities period, not an imminent operational deployment. The IRGC’s near-term Hormuz leverage is not threatened by this meeting. The morning brief overweighted this as a forcing function — it is not one this week. What it does do: it establishes the institutional architecture for a multilateral response, which matters on a 4-8 week horizon if the ugly-deal scenario materializes.

The Oil-Equity Divergence

This is the most important market signal of the day: S&P +1.05% to a new record AND Brent +3% above $100, on the same day. These are contradictory reads. Equities are pricing ceasefire de-escalation (the war ends, normalization follows). Oil is pricing extended Hormuz closure (IRGC seizures, gunfire, 5 ships transiting vs. 60 normal).

One of these reads is wrong. If Brent sustains above $100, inflation repricing will eventually hit equities — consumer squeeze, margin compression, and a Fed that can’t cut while oil is running. If Brent falls back below $95, the ceasefire-extension relief trade wins and equities were right all along. Watch overnight oil as the arbiter.
